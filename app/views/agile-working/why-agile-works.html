Why agile works

Agile methodologies will help you and your team to build world-class, user-centred services quickly and affordably.

  1. It minimises risk
  2. You can resolve problems early
  3. It’s cost effective
  4. It’s easier to change things
  5. You can experiment
  6. Planning is more efficient
  7. You’ll more productive
  8. It’s better for users

1. It minimises risk

Building a digital service is a complex task, with many risks. By breaking development into phases you minimise risk, learning about what works and what doesn't, iterating as you go.

By making regular releases of the service you’re building you reduce the risk of creating a ‘too-big-to-fail’ service that shouldn’t be released, but must be released anyway.

2. You can resolve problems early

Agile techniques don’t guarantee success – you can still fail!

But these techniques do allow you to spot problems earlier on and resolve them. You resolve issues, and stop issues from happening:

  1. if the product is wrong you can easily change direction and iterate
  2. if your teams’ speed is still inconsistent after the initial 4 to 6 sprints, then something needs fixing (possibly unknown complications or poor estimation with timings)
  3. if you highlight issues with quality early on you establish the issues, baseline the metrics, and monitor them throughout the project

    4. You don’t have to be afraid to fail or experiment. Learn to fail, and create a culture that learns from failure.

3. It’s cost effective

By working in phases you only build what you need to and you’ll only have the team members you need for each phase.

At the end of the discovery phase you’ll make a decision whether to proceed to the alpha phase based on your research and data.

If you decide there is a need for the service regularly releasing little pieces of the service will reduce cost to market.

4. It’s easier to change things

You shorten feedback loops that could be longer if using a waterfall methodology (where you only move on to the next phase when the phase you’re working on is complete). Producing regular iterations makes it easier for you to:

  1. add improvements
  2. fix bugs
  3. test expectations about your end product

5. You can experiment

You’ll be able to design and build a number of prototypes to test with end users, and throw them away. This gives the team freedom to explore a range of different concepts and gain a better understanding of what works best for end users.

6. Planning is more efficient

It’s a myth that you don’t plan on agile projects. The freedom of agile projects does not come free: you have to plan. You just plan differently and continuously.

Base your agile planning on solid, historical data, not theories or opinions. Your plan must continuously demonstrate its accuracy: nobody on your agile project should take it for granted that the plan is workable.

Working in phases allows the team making and operating the service to start small, learn fast, and provide value to users as soon as possible.

Working in sprints and doing regular user research lets the team think about what features are the next most important to produce and direct their efforts on creating usable features.

A good team means you’re able to estimate your output, or speed, very effectively and consistently. You can then plan much more accurately.

7. You’ll be more productive

Small agile teams of between 5 to 10 people are often more productive and predictable than larger teams. Forget man-days (the amount of work produced by an average worker in a day) and think about your team as a unit.

A good team includes members with all of the skills necessary to successfully build a service.

Working together is beneficial. 2 people working on 1 thing will:

  1. produce better software solutions
  2. encourage better quality controls
  3. spread knowledge across the team

8. It’s better for your users

Doing user research continuously will:

  1. keep your team concentrating on real user needs
  2. help teams design products which are prioritised by user needs
  3. help teams iterate products in response to user feedback
